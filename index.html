Corruption Perception Index

Corruption is often believed to be a significant barrier to economic growth. In contrast to the real levels of corruption, objective data on corruption primarily shows the success of anti-corruption programmes. A lower Corruption Perception Index or CPI score implies a higher level of perceived corruption, where bribery, extortion, and corruption are prevalent in business dealings, while a higher CPI score indicates a relatively clean government.

CPI Over the Years

The CPI generally moves gradually in an upward or downward direction, it rarely has a significant change. This is because CPI is a subjective indication of corruption and people take time to change their idea and opinion about something as important as their government and corruption. We can see that the only relatively significant change in the CPI has been with Mauritius, Canada and Iceland.

CPI in 2020

The Corruption Perception Index of the following countries shows how different countries population perceived their nation's officials in year 2020 and shows their trust on their government.

Infant Mortality

The correlation between infant mortality (per 1000 birth) and Corruption Perception Index is negative, which indicates that as healthcare system declines the government is considered more corrupt.

Literacy

The correlation between Literacy rate and Corruption Perception Index is somewhat positive, which indicates that as education system declines the government is considered more corrupt.

Gross Domestic Production

The correlation between Gross Domestic Production or GDP and Corruption Perception Index is positive, which indicates that as industries declines the government is considered more corrupt.

Underdeveloped Countries

We can see that there is a significantly larger change in the variation for underdeveloped nations. This is due to the fact that these nations have low CPIs, which suggest corrupt and unstable governments. As governments change through time, so do citizens' perceptions of those governments.
